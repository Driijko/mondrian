3.
PARIS
&
CUBISM
(1911 - 1914)

In 1911, age 39, Mondrian moved to Paris,
the thriving capital of European avant-garde culture.

There, he encountered the works of the Cubist painters Georges Braques and Pablo Picasso.

Like many of their contemporaries,
Braque and Picasso were impressed by the work of Paul Cezanne
and his emphasis on reducing figures to more basic forms.

Working from Cezanne's deconstructive approach to form,
Braques and Picasso developed an angular compositional style
that eventually came to be called "analytical cubism".

It resembled, one might say,
how a figure might look
if reflected in a shattered mirror.

Braques and Picasso increasingly treated the space of the scene
they were representing as a single texture,
rippling across the canvas surface.

It would seem that Braques and Picasso's work impressed Mondrian deeply,
for upon encountering it, he instantly "converted" to analytical cubism,
and away from the expressionist, fauvist, pointillist and symbolist styles
he had embraced during the spiritual awakening period.

Analytical cubism became his sole focus.

Mondrian did pursue certain themes from earlier,
such as ocean landscapes, and especially trees,
but with the lessons he had learned from Braques and Picasso.

Contorted through a cubist lense,
these figures become increasingly fragmented
and abstracted into geometric patterns.

Gone are the expressive and organic qualities of the spiritual awakening,
assimilated into grid-like compositions.

Gone also are the vibrant colors,
for, like Braques and Picasso,
and also similar to his Dutch Hague period,
Mondrian's cubism mostly utilized a muted color palette.
One of the more salient aspects of the analytical cubist style
was that it downplayed color contrasts
to make space for the style's experimental approach
to composition and form.

Mondrian's canvases lose their vivid colors of the last three years
and switch to subtle spectrums of white, grey, black, blue and yellowish-brown,
similar color palettes to Braques and Picasso's work.

At the same time, Mondrian did develop a unique approach to analytical cubism.
Rather than emphasizing space as a crumpled, fractured texture,
Mondrian increasingly emphasized flatness,
a reduction of the picture plane to a single flat surface.
Furthermore, while Braques and Picasso pursued busy, chaotic compositions,
Mondrian's paintings increasingly took on a calm and ordered sensibility.

In "flattening" the picture plane,
Mondrian was pursuing an experiment in avant-garde painting
that went back at least as far as Edouard Manet,
and which shows up in the work of the Impressionists,
Post-Impressionists,
and most of their avant-garde ilk.

Paul Cezanne is another signficant example.

But to fully flatten the picture plane was,
to some extent,
to abandon representationalism in painting all-together,
something that even the most daring of the avant-garde
were generally still reluctant to do.

However, Mondrian's take on analytical cubism
seems to have have led him inexorably towards abstraction.

By 1913, he was producing works whose representationalism
was largely implied by the titles,
but which would strike most people as abstract paintings.

Mondrian became increasingly obssessed
with a grid-like compositional style
on a fully flat picture plane.

His works at this point almost resemble stained glass windows,
but with no discernible figures or scenes.

More colorful palettes re-appear.

In 1913, Mondrian became one of the first
in the European avant-garde painting world
to fully embrace abstraction.

His works had shifted from analytical cubism
to an early example of geometrical abstraction.

Once again,
Mondrian had completely mutated his approach to painting,
his analytical cubist phase being different in many significant ways
to the spriritual awakening period.

Furthermore, within less than two years,
Mondrian had pushed his analytical cubist style
into what can be called his early abstract style.

And Mondrian's transformation was hardly over.

In 1914, World War I was on the horizon,
a war which would reshape European art,
and which would see the modernist avant-garde
explode in innovation and experimentation.

WWI was a horrific and traumatizing experience for Europe.
This time of crisis caused the avant-garde
to become increasingly bold and critical of the status quo.

Throughout Europe, a sense of radical change was everywhere.
