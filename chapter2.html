In 1908, age 36, Mondrian came under the influence of theosophy and anthroposophy.

These two ideologies emerged in the late 19th century and reflected a shift in Western spirituality away from Christian religious authority towards a more global, syncretic, and esoteric viewpoint, very much anticipating contemporary new age culture.

In 1909, Mondrian joined the Dutch branch of the Theosophical Society, and would later comment on the profound impact that Helena Blavatsky's book, The Secret Doctrine, had on his thinking and work. He also wrote to Rudolf Steiner, sharing his own ideas about spirituality and art.

Mondrian's spiritual awakening triggered a major shift in his work.

His canvases suddenly burst with color and a more expressive technique.

He absorbed a variety of styles,
including pointillism...

fauvism...

expressionism...

...and symbolism.

The influence of artists such as
Edvard Munch and Andre Derain
are evident in this period.

There were some points of continuity between Mondrian's earlier work and his work of this period: landscape painting of rural scenes...

...and a fascination with windmills.

Despite certain aspects of continuity with his early work,
the period from 1908 to 1911 is much more a break from the past than an extension of it. Mondrian's color palette completely transforms,
the expressive qualities of his work become heightened,
and he embraced the most cutting edge aesthetics of the contemporary avant-garde.
It seems that Mondrian's encounter with theosophy
had a profoundly liberating effect on his relationship with painting.
There is a dynamic and vital quality in his work from this period
quite unlike anything that had come before.

Other important themes from this period include trees...

...flowers...

...and portraiture.

One particular series of sand dune landscapes showed Mondrian approaching complete abstraction, an anticipation of his later aesthetic.

Some call the period of 1908 - 1911 Mondrian's expressionist period, and it certainly showcases a more expressive style than his work under the influence of the Dutch Hague school.

At the same time, Mondrian was also incorporating the more formalist qualities of fauvism and pointillism, so it would be wrong to classify this period as purely expressionist.

Whether in the pursuit of more expressive or more formal affects, a consistent aspect of Mondrian's work from this period is the vivid and, at times, explosive use of color.

But perhaps the best way to typify this period is by recognizing the underlying connection to Mondrian's encounter with theosophy. This, above all, is the period of Mondrian's "spiritual awakening". His enthusiasm for theosophy comes to life in his work.

For those more familiar with Mondrian's later work,
encountering the work from the spiritual awakening period can be somewhat startling. Mondrian later became famous for a style that was rigorously committed
to simple color schemes and rectangular geometry,
with a rather austere sensibility.
The colorful and vital qualities of his work from 1908 to 1911
stand in stark contrast to his later work,
and indeed it forms a rather singular and distinctive moment in his career as an artist.

In 1911, Mondrian's life was once again on the precipice of transformative change,
and, as had happened 3 years earlier,
his art and aesthetic were about to dramatically reshape themselves
into new and unforseen directions.
