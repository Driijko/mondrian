6.
The
Late
Style (1932 - 1944)

The stylistic shifts
in Mondrian's work
during the 1930s
can be considered
a transition
between his mature style
and his late style.

Compositions become more dense,
and space feels more constrained.

There is less emphasis on asymmetry,
and more on the tension created by
overlapping and parallel lines.

These changes anticipate
the more dramatic
stylistic shifts
of Mondrian's late style.

In 1938,
with Mondrian now age 66,
the second World War was looming.

Knowing that Paris was in danger,
Mondrian moved briefly to London,
and then to New York City in 1940.

It is during this time
that Mondrian's late style
really emerges,
as he sheds off
some of the constraints
of his previous work.

Colors lose their black borders,
and become more plentiful.

The austerity and starkness
of the mature style
gives way to a sense
of vitality and energy.

In particular,
Mondrian seemed in awe of New York City,
the rising emblem of modern urbanity,
and also by jazz,
a music that was
spontaneous, intricate,
and rhythmically exciting.

It seems, in his final days,
that Mondrian desired to break his own rules;
to embrace a more playful and vibrant aesthetic.

Two of his last works,
Broad-way Boogie Woogie
and the unfinished Victory Boogie-Woogie,
show a dynamic, jubilant quality
that is a significant departure from his work in Paris.

It's interesting to ponder,
had he lived longer,
where these experiments
would have led him.
